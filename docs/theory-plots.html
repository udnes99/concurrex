Pure mathematical plots for each section of THEORY.md. Compare against the live simulation.

§2. ProDel: Probabilistic Delay Load-shedding

§2.3 Drop Probability: P(s) = 1 − τ/s

Each stale entry (sojourn ≥ τ) is dropped with probability proportional to how far past the threshold it is. At s = τ: P = 0 (just crossed, give it a chance). At s = 2τ: P = 0.5 (coin flip). At s = 10τ: P = 0.9. Smooth gradient — no hard cutoff.

§2.3 Drop Schedule: W / √nd

Time between drop evaluation rounds decreases as cumulative drops nd grow. First round fires immediately. After 100 drops, rounds fire every W/10. The 1/√n schedule accelerates shedding under sustained overload.

§3. Probabilistic Early Shedding

§3.1 Early Shed Probability

P = (δ/(δ+μ)) × shrinkage(μ), where δ is the drop rate EWMA and μ is the completion rate EWMA. Shown for various drop-to-completion ratios. The shrinkage factor dampens P at low throughput.

§4. Throughput Regulator

§4.1 Time-Weighted EWMA Alpha

α = 1 − exp(−Δt / (HALF_LIFE × W)). Shown for HALF_LIFE = 9 and W = 100ms. On-time windows (Δt = 100ms) get α ≈ 0.105. Long-delayed windows get more weight (staler data discounted).

§4.1.1 Bayesian Shrinkage: n/(n + Z²)

Weights an observation of n samples against a prior of Z² = 4 pseudo-observations. At n = 1: 20% trust in new data. At n = 4: 50%. At n = 100: 96%. Same denominator as the Wilson score interval.

§4.2 WoLF: IMQ Weight Function

w = 1/√(1 + ν²/Z²). The Inverse Multiquadric (IMQ) kernel downweights outlier innovations. At ν = 0: full weight (w = 1). At ν = Z = 2: w = 1/√2 ≈ 0.71 (half-weight point in variance terms). At ν = 6: w ≈ 0.32. Never reaches zero — even extreme outliers get some weight.

§4.2 WoLF: Effective Kalman Gain vs Innovation

Keff = K × w, showing how the Kalman gain drops for outlier innovations. At steady state with full shrinkage, K ≈ α0 ≈ 0.105 for clean observations. An outlier at ν = 4 gets Keff ≈ 0.03 — barely moves the filtered state. Three curves show the effect of shrinkage (1, 4, and 20 completions).

§4.3.1 Variance Convergence Inflation

SE is inflated by 1/√shrinkage(elapsedWindows/HALF_LIFE) to account for the Welford variance not having converged. After 1 half-life: 2.24× inflation. After 5 half-lives: 1.34×. After 20: 1.10×. Prevents false positives from an underestimated variance during early operation.

§4.4 Step Formula: Increase f(d) vs Retraction f/(1+f)

Both increase and retraction use the convergent factor f(d) = 1 − exp(−d/HALF_LIFE). Increase multiplies L by (1+f); retraction divides by (1+f) using f/(1+f). At convergence (d → ∞): increase doubles (f → 1), retraction halves (f/(1+f) → 0.5). Retraction is the exact multiplicative inverse of increase.

§4.4 Convergent Step Sizes (L = 100)

Absolute step sizes for increase, retraction, and fresh decrease at L = 100. Increase and fresh decrease use the same formula but grow at different rates because L changes between steps. Retraction uses f/(1+f) for exact inverse.

§4.4 Bisection Convergence (stepScale)

Each increase→retract→cooling cycle halves stepScale. The effective first-step size shrinks exponentially: 10.5%, 5.3%, 2.6%, 1.3%, ... After k cycles, the oscillation band is ≤ L × 0.105 / 2k. Convergence to within ±1 in O(log L) cycles.

§4.6 Welford Variance Bias: 2(1−α)/(2−α)

The Welford EWMS underestimates the true process variance σ². At α = 0.105 (HALF_LIFE = 9): bias factor ≈ 0.945 (5.5% underestimate). The SE formula corrects for this via the (2(1−α)) denominator.

Per-Lane Error Rate: Bayesian Shrinkage

Bayesian shrinkage factor n/(n+Z²) used to dampen per-lane error rate EWMA updates for lanes with few completions. Conservative at low counts (1–3 completions), converges to 1 at high counts.
